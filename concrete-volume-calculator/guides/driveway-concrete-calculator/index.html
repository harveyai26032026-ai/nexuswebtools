Concrete Calculator for Driveways: Cost and Volume Estimation

A concrete driveway is a significant investment — typically the largest single concrete pour on a residential property. Getting the volume, thickness and cost estimation right before you order saves money, prevents short loads, and ensures the slab performs for decades. This guide covers every factor you need to consider.

Step 1: Measure the driveway area

Driveways come in several shapes. Calculate the area depending on the layout:

  • Rectangular (most common): Area = Length × Width. A 6 m × 3 m driveway = 18 m².
  • T-shaped or flared: Split into rectangles and triangles. Calculate each section separately and add them together.
  • Curved: Approximate with a series of rectangles, or measure the average width and multiply by the length.
  • On a slope: The area is the horizontal plan area, not the sloped surface area. The slope only affects the thickness slightly — the volume difference is negligible for typical residential gradients.

Step 2: Choose the right thickness

Driveway thickness is more critical than for any other residential slab because it must support vehicle loads:

Vehicle typeMinimum thicknessRecommended
Cars and SUVs only100 mm (4″)125 mm (5″)
Light trucks, vans125 mm (5″)150 mm (6″)
Heavy vehicles, RVs150 mm (6″)175 mm (7″)

The extra 25 mm from 100 to 125 mm increases concrete volume by 25% but roughly doubles the load capacity. For most homes, 125 mm is the sweet spot.

Step 3: Calculate the volume

Volume = Area × Thickness

For a 6 m × 3 m driveway at 125 mm:

Volume = 18 × 0.125 = 2.25 m³

Add 5% waste: 2.25 × 1.05 = 2.36 m³

At this volume, ready-mix delivery is strongly recommended over bagged concrete (you'd need ~236 × 20 kg bags).

If you're hiring a contractor for excavation, formwork, pouring and finishing, add $60–$120/m² in labour — bringing the total to roughly $2,200–$3,300 for an 18 m² driveway.

Step 5: Base preparation

The sub-base is just as important as the concrete itself. A poorly prepared base will settle, causing the slab to crack and shift:

  1. Excavate to a depth of slab thickness + sub-base depth (e.g., 125 + 75 = 200 mm below finished level).
  2. Lay geotextile fabric over the subgrade to prevent the sub-base mixing with the soil.
  3. Spread and compact road base (75–100 mm) in 50 mm layers using a plate compactor. Each layer should be compacted to at least 95% modified Proctor density.
  4. Set up formwork with 50 × 100 mm timber, staked at 600 mm intervals. The top of the formwork sets the finished slab level.
  5. Place reinforcement — F72 mesh (6 mm bars at 200 mm centres) on 20 mm bar chairs. Wire all laps. For heavy vehicle use, consider SL82 mesh (8 mm bars).
  6. Schedule the pour — concrete must be placed within 90 minutes of batching. Have at least two people on site for a driveway pour.

Step 6: Drainage considerations

A driveway that traps water will deteriorate quickly. Ensure a minimum 1% fall (1:100) away from the house or toward a drain. For a 6 m driveway, that's a 60 mm drop from one end to the other. If the natural slope doesn't allow this, consider a surface drain (trench grate) across the driveway at the low point.

Common driveway concrete mistakes

  • Skimping on thickness — 100 mm is the bare minimum for cars. Hairline cracks become structural cracks under load.
  • No sub-base — pouring directly on soil or clay guarantees settlement cracks within 1–2 years.
  • No control joints — saw cut within 4–18 hours of pouring (see our saw cut guide).
  • Adding water on site — suppliers may ask if you want to "wet it up". Say no — every litre of extra water reduces strength.
  • Pouring in extreme heat — above 32°C, concrete sets too fast and is prone to plastic shrinkage cracks. Pour early morning in summer.
