How to Calculate Concrete for a Shed Slab

A shed slab is one of the most common DIY concrete projects — and also one where people most often order the wrong amount of concrete. Order too little and you're left with a half-poured slab; order too much and you've wasted money. This guide walks through every step of the calculation so you get it right the first time.

Step 1: Measure the slab dimensions

Before you can calculate anything, you need three measurements:

  • Length — the longest side of the slab, measured in metres or feet.
  • Width — the shorter side, perpendicular to the length.
  • Thickness — how deep the slab will be, measured from the finished surface down to the base material.

For a typical garden shed (3 m × 2 m), the slab footprint is simply 3 × 2 = 6 m². But thickness is where many people go wrong — read on.

Step 2: Choose the right thickness

The thickness of your shed slab depends on what you're storing and the soil conditions:

Shed typeRecommended thicknessNotes
Garden tool shed (light)100 mm (4″)Standard for most residential sheds
Workshop / heavy storage125–150 mm (5–6″)Supports machinery and point loads
Vehicular access shed150 mm (6″)If a vehicle or ride-on mower enters

When in doubt, go thicker. The cost difference between 100 mm and 125 mm is modest — typically an extra 25% on concrete volume — but the strength gain is significant.

Step 3: Calculate the volume

The formula is straightforward:

Volume = Length × Width × Thickness

For a 3 m × 2 m shed slab at 100 mm thick:

Volume = 3 × 2 × 0.10 = 0.60 m³

In imperial units, a 10 ft × 8 ft slab at 4 inches thick:

Volume = 10 × 8 × (4/12) = 26.7 ft³ = 0.99 yd³

Step 4: Add a waste allowance

Concrete doesn't always go exactly where you plan. Spillage, uneven sub-base, over-excavation and formwork gaps all eat into your supply. The industry standard is to add 5–10% extra:

  • 5% — for a well-prepared site with accurate formwork and experienced pourers.
  • 10% — for DIY projects, uneven ground, or when mixing bagged concrete by hand.

For our 0.60 m³ example with 5% waste:

0.60 × 1.05 = 0.63 m³

Step 5: Estimate bag count (if using bagged concrete)

For small slabs, bagged concrete is often more practical than ready-mix delivery. Here's a quick reference:

Bag sizeYield per bagBags for 0.63 m³
20 kg≈ 0.010 m³≈ 63 bags
40 kg≈ 0.020 m³≈ 32 bags
80 lb≈ 0.60 ft³≈ 37 bags (for ~22 ft³)

At 60+ bags of 20 kg, consider ready-mix delivery — it's usually cheaper and far less labour-intensive for volumes above 0.5 m³.

Step 6: Site preparation tips

A well-prepared site makes the pour go smoothly and prevents cracking:

  1. Excavate to depth — dig down to allow for sub-base (50–75 mm of compacted gravel) plus slab thickness. For a 100 mm slab on 75 mm of road base, excavate 175 mm below finished ground level.
  2. Compact the sub-base — use crushed rock or road base, compacted in layers with a plate compactor. A soft or uneven base is the #1 cause of slab cracks.
  3. Set up formwork — use straight, rigid timber (50 × 100 mm or 2×4) staked at 600 mm intervals. Check diagonally to ensure the slab is square.
  4. Lay reinforcement — for sheds, a sheet of F62 or F72 mesh on 20 mm bar chairs is sufficient. Wire the overlaps and keep the mesh in the top third of the slab.
  5. Order concrete — call the supplier 24–48 hours ahead. Specify the strength (25 MPa is standard for shed slabs), the total volume including waste, and the delivery time.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Forgetting the sub-base depth — your excavation depth is not the slab thickness; it's slab thickness plus sub-base.
  • Ordering exactly the calculated volume — always round up or add waste allowance. Running short mid-pour creates a cold joint that weakens the slab.
  • Using the wrong strength mix — a garden shed can use 20 MPa, but a workshop with heavy machinery should be 32 MPa.
  • Placing mesh incorrectly — mesh sitting on the base does nothing. It must be chaired up into the top third of the slab.
