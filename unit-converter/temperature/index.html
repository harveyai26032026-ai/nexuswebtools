Background

Temperature measures the thermal energy of a substance. Three scales dominate: Celsius (used worldwide for everyday purposes), Fahrenheit (used in the US for weather and cooking), and Kelvin (used in science and engineering, where zero represents absolute zero). Understanding temperature conversion is essential for cooking, science, weather interpretation, and international communication.

The Three Temperature Scales

Celsius sets water freezing at 0° and boiling at 100°. Fahrenheit sets water freezing at 32° and boiling at 212°. Kelvin starts at absolute zero (−273.15°C) and uses the same degree spacing as Celsius. Each scale was developed for different reasons: Celsius for scientific convenience, Fahrenheit for precision in everyday weather, and Kelvin for thermodynamic calculations.

Absolute Zero and Kelvin

Absolute zero (0 K or −273.15°C) is the theoretical lowest possible temperature, where molecular motion ceases. The Kelvin scale is used in physics and chemistry because it directly represents thermal energy without negative numbers.

Applications

Cooking and Baking

Oven temperatures in recipes vary by country. An American recipe might specify 350°F, which converts to 175°C (actually 176.7°C, but ovens use 25°C increments). Converting correctly is critical for baking success.

Weather and Travel

When travelling between metric and imperial countries, converting temperature helps you understand weather forecasts and dress appropriately. 30°C is a hot day (86°F), while 0°C is freezing (32°F).

Science and Engineering

Scientific calculations often require Kelvin. Room temperature (22°C) is 295.15 K. Thermodynamic equations, gas laws, and material properties all use the Kelvin scale.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you convert Celsius to Fahrenheit?

Multiply the Celsius temperature by 9/5, then add 32. For example, 25°C × 9/5 + 32 = 77°F. Our converter does this instantly and shows the formula used.

What temperature is the same in Celsius and Fahrenheit?

−40°C equals −40°F. This is the only point where the two scales intersect. It is extremely cold — roughly the temperature of a harsh Antarctic winter.

Why does the US use Fahrenheit?

The US adopted Fahrenheit in the 18th century through British colonial influence and never switched to Celsius. The Fahrenheit scale offers finer granularity for weather reporting (each degree is smaller than a Celsius degree), which some argue makes it more intuitive for describing outdoor temperatures.
