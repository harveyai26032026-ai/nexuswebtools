Background

Investment growth is driven by compound returns — when your earnings (dividends, capital gains, interest) are reinvested, they generate their own earnings. This compounding effect is what turns modest, consistent contributions into substantial wealth over long periods. The Australian share market has returned an average of approximately 9.8% per annum over 30 years, with dividends reinvested.

The Power of Reinvested Dividends

Dividends may seem small — typically 3–5% yield on Australian shares — but when reinvested, they dramatically accelerate compounding. A $10,000 investment in the ASX 200 with dividends reinvested would have grown to approximately $92,000 over 25 years, compared to approximately $58,000 without dividend reinvestment.

Dollar-Cost Averaging and Compounding

Investing a fixed amount at regular intervals (dollar-cost averaging) combines naturally with compound interest. You buy more units when prices are low and fewer when high, while earlier purchases have more time to compound. This strategy reduces timing risk and maximises long-term compound growth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a realistic return on investments?

For diversified Australian shares, a long-term average of 7–9% per annum (including reinvested dividends) is reasonable. International shares may return 8–10%. Bonds typically return 4–6%. These are historical averages — actual returns vary year to year and can be negative in any given year.

Should I reinvest my dividends?

Yes, for long-term growth. Reinvested dividends are a major driver of compound returns. Over 25 years, dividend reinvestment can add 50%+ to your total return compared to taking dividends as cash.

How does market volatility affect compound returns?

Short-term volatility has minimal impact on long-term compound returns. Markets have historically recovered from every downturn. In fact, regular contributions during downturns buy more units at lower prices, which enhances long-term compounding (dollar-cost averaging).

What is the difference between nominal and real investment returns?

Nominal returns are the raw percentage your investment grows. Real returns subtract inflation. If your portfolio grows 8% and inflation is 3%, your real return is approximately 5%. Always consider real returns for long-term planning.
