Background

Compound interest is the process where interest earned on your savings is added to your balance, and future interest is calculated on the new, larger amount. Unlike simple interest — which is always calculated on the original deposit — compound interest creates exponential growth over time. The concept dates back to 17th-century mathematics and remains the foundation of modern savings and banking.

The Mathematics Behind Savings Growth

The compound interest formula is A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where P is the principal, r is the annual interest rate, n is the compounding frequency, and t is the time in years. For regular deposits, each contribution begins compounding from the moment it is added, which is why consistent savers see accelerating growth over time.

Why Compounding Frequency Matters

The more often interest compounds, the more you earn. A $10,000 deposit at 5% annual interest yields $10,500 with annual compounding but $10,511.62 with daily compounding after one year. Over 30 years, this small difference becomes hundreds of dollars — entirely free.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is compound interest calculated on savings?

Compound interest on savings is calculated using the formula A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where P is your initial deposit, r is the annual interest rate, n is the number of times interest compounds per year, and t is the number of years. Our calculator handles this automatically — just enter your details.

What is a good interest rate for a savings account?

High-yield savings accounts typically offer 4–5% APY as of 2025, compared to 0.5–1% for standard accounts at major banks. The difference on $10,000 over 10 years is thousands of dollars due to compounding. Online banks generally offer higher rates than brick-and-mortar institutions.

Is it better to compound monthly or daily?

Daily compounding produces slightly higher returns than monthly. On $10,000 at 5% for 10 years: daily yields approximately $16,486 vs monthly approximately $16,470. The difference is small but free — choose daily compounding when available.

How much should I save each month?

A common guideline is to save at least 20% of your income. For specific goals, use the calculator to work backwards: enter your target amount, time horizon, and interest rate, then adjust the monthly contribution until the projected balance matches your goal.

Does inflation affect my savings?

Yes. If your savings earn 5% and inflation is 3%, your real return is approximately 2%. Our calculator shows nominal growth — for inflation-adjusted projections, use a lower effective interest rate (e.g. 2% instead of 5%).
