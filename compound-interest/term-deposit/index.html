Background

A term deposit is a fixed-term investment where you lock away a lump sum at a guaranteed interest rate for a set period (typically 1 month to 5 years). Unlike savings accounts where rates can change at any time, term deposits offer certainty — you know exactly what you will earn. Understanding how compounding works within a term deposit helps you choose the right product and reinvestment strategy.

How Term Deposit Interest Is Paid

Term deposits typically pay interest monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, or at maturity. When interest is paid at maturity, it does not compound within the term — you receive a simple interest calculation. When interest is paid periodically and reinvested, compounding occurs. This distinction significantly affects your total return.

The Reinvestment Risk

When a term deposit matures, you must reinvest at the prevailing rate. If rates have fallen, your next term may earn less. This reinvestment risk is a key consideration when choosing between short and long terms.

How to Use This Calculator

Use our Compound Interest Calculator to compare term deposit options:

  1. Enter the term deposit amount as the principal

    This is the lump sum you plan to invest. Most term deposits require a minimum of $1,000–$5,000.

  2. Set zero for regular contributions

    Term deposits do not accept additional deposits during the term. Leave the contribution field at $0.

  3. Enter the advertised interest rate

    Use the annual rate quoted by the bank. Ensure you are comparing the same compounding basis across products.

  4. Set the compounding frequency

    Choose monthly, quarterly, or at maturity depending on the product. At maturity = no compounding (simple interest). Monthly or quarterly = compound interest.

  5. Set the term in years

    Convert months to years: 6 months = 0.5 years, 12 months = 1 year, 24 months = 2 years.

  6. Compare multiple products

    Run separate calculations for each term deposit product to compare total returns. Pay attention to compounding frequency — a lower rate with monthly compounding can outperform a higher rate with annual compounding.

Applications

Term deposit compound interest calculations help with several financial decisions:

Rate and Term Comparison

Compare 6-month vs 12-month vs 24-month terms at different rates. Sometimes a shorter term at a slightly lower rate, combined with expected rate rises, produces a better overall return than locking in a long term now.

Laddering Strategy

Divide your investment across multiple term deposits with staggered maturity dates (e.g. $10K at 6 months, $10K at 12 months, $10K at 24 months). This provides regular access to funds and reduces reinvestment risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do term deposits compound interest?

It depends on the product. If interest is paid at maturity, it is simple interest — no compounding. If interest is paid monthly or quarterly and reinvested, it compounds. Always check the interest payment frequency when comparing term deposits.

Is a term deposit better than a savings account?

Term deposits offer a guaranteed rate for a fixed period, which is valuable when rates are falling. Savings accounts offer flexibility but variable rates. For money you will not need for 6–24 months, term deposits often offer slightly higher rates in exchange for locking your funds.
