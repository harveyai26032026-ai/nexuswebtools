Background

Retirement planning is the quintessential application of compound interest. The difference between starting at age 25 versus 35 can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars more in retirement savings — even if the later starter contributes more in total. Time is the single most powerful variable in retirement calculations because compound interest needs years to produce its exponential effect.

The Cost of Delaying Retirement Savings

If you save $500/month at 7% annual return starting at age 25, you will have approximately $1,224,000 by age 65. If you start at 35 with the same contributions, you will have approximately $567,000 — less than half. The 10-year delay costs you $657,000 despite only missing $60,000 in actual contributions.

Superannuation and Compound Interest

In Australia, employer superannuation contributions (11.5% as of 2024–25) are invested and compound over your entire working life. For someone earning $90,000, employer contributions alone can grow to over $500,000 after 30 years, with compound interest accounting for roughly 60% of the final balance.

How to Use This Calculator

Our Compound Interest Calculator is ideal for retirement projections. Set it up as follows:

  1. Enter your current retirement savings balance

    Include superannuation, investment accounts, and any other retirement-focused savings.

  2. Enter your regular contribution

    Include both your personal contributions and employer superannuation contributions. For example, on a $90,000 salary with 11.5% SG, that is approximately $860/month from your employer alone.

  3. Set the contribution frequency to monthly

    Most retirement contributions are made monthly via payroll, so this gives the most accurate projection.

  4. Enter an expected annual return

    A balanced super fund historically returns 7–8% per annum. Use 7% for a conservative estimate or 5% for a very conservative projection.

  5. Set the time horizon to years until retirement

    If you are 35 and plan to retire at 65, enter 30 years. Adjust based on your target retirement age.

  6. Use scenario comparison for before/after analysis

    Run one scenario with current contributions and another with increased contributions (e.g. salary sacrifice) to see the impact of voluntary super contributions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I save for retirement?

A common guideline is to save 15% of your pre-tax income for retirement, including employer contributions. The exact amount depends on your desired lifestyle, expected retirement age, and investment returns. Use the calculator to model different scenarios and find the right savings rate for your goals.

How does compound interest help retirement savings?

Compound interest means your investment returns generate their own returns. Over 30–40 years of saving, compound interest typically accounts for more than half of your final retirement balance. This is why starting early is so important — each additional year of compounding adds significantly to your final balance.

What return rate should I use for retirement projections?

For Australian balanced super funds, a long-term average of 7% per annum is reasonable. For conservative estimates, use 5%. For aggressive growth portfolios, 8–9% may be appropriate but comes with higher volatility. Always factor in that past returns do not guarantee future performance.

How does inflation affect my retirement savings?

Inflation reduces the purchasing power of your savings. If your investments return 7% and inflation averages 3%, your real return is approximately 4%. For inflation-adjusted projections, use a lower effective return rate in the calculator (e.g. 4% instead of 7%).
