Background

Student loans can accrue significant compound interest over the life of the loan, especially if repayments are deferred or only minimum payments are made. In Australia, HECS-HELP loans are indexed to inflation rather than charged commercial interest, but the compounding effect of indexation still increases the debt over time. For private student loans or overseas loans with commercial interest rates, the compounding effect is much more pronounced.

HECS-HELP Indexation vs Compound Interest

Australian HECS-HELP loans do not charge interest in the traditional sense. Instead, they are indexed annually to inflation (CPI). In 2023, indexation was 7.1% — higher than many commercial interest rates. While technically not compound interest, indexation compounds because it is applied to the total debt including previously indexed amounts.

Private Student Loan Compounding

Private student loans and international student loans typically charge 5–12% interest that compounds during the repayment period. Some loans even compound during the study period (capitalised interest), meaning you graduate with a larger balance than you borrowed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does HECS-HELP charge compound interest?

No, but it is indexed annually to inflation (CPI), which has a similar compounding effect. Indexation is applied to the total debt including previously indexed amounts, so the debt grows exponentially if repayments do not keep pace.

Should I pay off my student loan early?

For HECS-HELP, it depends on the indexation rate versus what you could earn by investing the money instead. When indexation is low (2–3%), investing typically outperforms early repayment. When indexation is high (5%+), voluntary repayment becomes more attractive. For private loans with high interest rates, early repayment almost always saves money.
